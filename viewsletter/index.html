Viewsletter #1 - Visualizing Elections

Welcome to Viewsletter, your monthly data visualization newsletter. Each issue, we explore different themes to show how thoughtful visualization choices can transform data into clear and compelling insights. This month we focus on how data visualizations can be used to tell stories about elections. From colorful maps to complex graphs, we’ll see how different visualization techniques can be used to effectively represent electoral data.

2024 has been a year full of elections. Indonesia, India, UK, France, Mexico, Iran, United States, South Africa, Thailand, the European Parliament… As the timeline published by Reuters shows, most of the world's major democracies — totalling almost half of the world’s population — have had or will have general elections until the end of the year.


Source: Reuters

Every election is different, and such a wide array of countries will obviously have different political contexts, electoral systems, and voting issues. For example, we take for granted that having an election means that there will be a day in which everyone in a country can vote. Well… that’s not always the case. Take the example of India, whose general elections can last for months, as different regions vote on different days.

(Questionable color choice) Source: CNN World

This heterogeneity also means that there is a huge diversity of data visualization problems to tackle. In fact, even for the same election, very different techniques can be used to represent the same data. Many of those choices are deliberate and guide narratives in a certain, intentional way. As an example, the Thai website WeVIS did an amazing job mapping different visualization choices of several websites covering the Thai elections. (I suggest using the Safari in-browser translation function, as it also translates the text labels in images).

Source: WeVIS

Empty land doesn’t vote!

One of the biggest challenges in election coverage is effectively representing the proportion of voters for each candidate on traditional maps. Standard Mercator projected maps often fall short, particularly in areas where population density varies significantly. When vast, sparsely populated regions visually dominate the map, they can inadvertently suggest an overwhelming support for a candidate, overshadowing densely populated areas where the majority of voters actually reside. This creates a misleading narrative, highlighting the necessity for alternative visualization techniques that more accurately reflect voter distribution.

Source: engaging-data.com

In the United Kingdom, the parliamentary electoral system is structured around constituencies that are roughly equal in terms of population but can differ dramatically in geographic size. This means that while each Member of Parliament represents approximately the same number of constituents, the physical areas of these constituencies can range from very small urban districts to vast rural regions. Furthermore, the UK uses a "first past the post" (FPTP) voting system, in which the candidate who receives the most votes in a constituency wins the seat, regardless of whether they achieve an outright majority. To visualize the election results it is important then to represent constituencies consistently using the same visual units.

In the case of Bloomberg’s article about the elections, this was achieved by using a square grid in which each square represents a constituency. The squares' positions are relative to the geographic position of each other. This results in a map that clearly communicates the election results, but that is far from an accurate geographical representation of the United Kingdom.

Source: Bloomberg

A similar example of using a grid to represent constituency results can be found in Le Monde's article about the French legislative election results. In this case, a hexagonal grid is used alongside icons that indicate whether a constituency has shifted towards a nearby or distant political family.

Source: Le Monde

An example workaround of this issue is The Economist’s UK election forecast maps. In this case, a geographically accurate map of Britain is used, with the constituencies colored by the winning party. However, that map is layered on top by a set of circles that are in the closest possible position of the geometric center of the constituencies. Obviously, in denser regions (like London), this results in the circles being not even on top of their own constituencies, but the clever circle hover interaction that highlights the borders of the respective constituency solves this problem by providing a clear connection between circle and geographic entity. Interestingly, the article then focuses on specific regions, guiding the reader on a more in-depth analysis of the maps.

Source: The Economist

The previously mentioned WeVIS article on online coverage of the Thai elections provides an interesting comparison between representations of results in a map. It divides maps that use the geographic area of constituencies and those that use the same visual unit for all constituencies. This last category is further divided by the shape of the visual unit used (circle, square or diamond).

Polls and uncertainty

When it comes to understanding elections, interpreting poll data and communicating uncertainty and possible outcomes is central. However, journalistic coverage about polls still falls many times in over simplistic conclusions like “Candidate X has an advantage over Y” without giving odds to the outcomes, or “Party Z has n% of the votes”, when in fact vote shares from polls are ranges, and what is highly probable is that the results will fall into those ranges. (For more about this, I recommend Nate Silver’s book).

FiveThirtyEight is usually referred to as a good example when it comes to statistical communication. In their article about the US 2024 Presidential Election, outcome distribution is central. 100 simulations are performed and then arranged horizontally following an electoral vote margin axis. It is easy to understand that outcomes resulting from more simulations have bigger odds of happening than cases that appear less often.

Source: 538

The Economist opted for a simplified version of a boxplot, with lowest, highest and median outcome results for each party, color hues representing probability, an indication of number of MPs needed for majority, and a comparison with last election’s results. Instead of telling a story around the median result, sometimes mistaken as the most probable result, ranges and outcome probabilities are central in this visualization.

Source: The Economist

An useful exercise that is often overlooked is to compare poll forecasts with the actual election results. In the article Which German party is the most unlucky when it comes to election dates? by Datawrapper, a graph for each party shows the difference between average forecasted voting share and actual voting share in the following election. The graphs are annotated with useful information about events that might have contributed to variations in this difference.

Source: Datawrapper

Change

When it comes to visualizing change, the array of visualization solutions is almost infinite, depending on the angle of analysis or story that is intended to be told. Talking about change in the number of elected officials instead of differences in voting share can almost turn a sweeping victory into a disappointing defeat. The same can happen when we look at individual parties instead of political blocks; or when we look at specific regions instead of at a country as a whole (or vice-versa).

A very simple example is the one of Público’s 2024 Portuguese Elections. It goes straight to the point, showing differences in the number of MPs in relation with the previous election. This approach is valid and useful, but can also be too simplistic if not accompanied with other sets of information.

Source: Público

Bloomberg’s coverage of the UK Elections shows a similar example, but about vote share instead of number of elected MPs.

Source: Bloomberg

A different example is Bloomberg’s coverage of the recent French elections. In this case, we can see not only the evolution in terms of representation of each party since 2012, but also how the political blocks (or coalitions) they integrate have evolved. For example, if we looked only into France Unbowed’s numbers, we could get to the conclusion that they stagnated from 2022 to 2024. However, it misses the context in which they integrate (and lead) a coalition that kept growing during that period. On the other hand, we see that the unquestionable growth of the far-right has been happening at the expense of the right-wing Republicans, and that the sum of both is still less than what it was in 2012.

Source: Bloomberg

The New York Times coverage of the UK elections gives us an even more clear and intentional perspective on the downfall of a party (in this example, the Conservatives) in relation to the rise of others (Reform UK). This chart related Conservative’s vote shift with Reform’s voting share, with the help of suggestive downward arrows.

Source: The New York Times

Bloomberg also focuses on the rise of Reform UK. Showing how close Conservatives came to hold a seat relating the margin of loss with the third parties vote share is a way to suggest the theory that if Reform didn’t run, the Conservatives would have had a much better result.  

Source: Bloomberg

Finally, by looking at change from a spatial perspective, it is possible to uncover trends that would be undetectable otherwise. A common way to look at this is by using the wind map metaphor, inspired by an older New York Times visualization that became very popular due to its simplicity: blue leftward arrows mean that a location has moved its vote to the left, and red rightward ones mean that it has moved its vote to the right (due to opposite political color conventions, the colors and directions have to be swapped in the rest of the world). The size/velocity (and sometimes color intensity) of the arrow is proportional to the size of the vote shift.

This metaphor is still widely used, as we can see in the example of the recent UK elections coverage of The New York Times (in this case, arrows are pointing down perhaps to represent downfall).

Source: The New York Times

Perhaps the map of the Belgian shifts between the 2019 and 2024 European Elections by Karim Douieb is a richer example, since instead of just showing a general national trend, it uncovers specific and very defined regional trends. As Belgium is made up of three very different political entities (Flanders, Wallonia and Brussels) with separated political parties, it is interesting to note that the ones typically more on the left side of the spectrum, Wallonia and Brussels, have moved very noticeably to the right, while the historically right-wing Flanders has moved to the left, even if not in the same degree.

Source: @karim_douieb

More maps!

There is no question that spatial variables are of huge importance when it comes to political identification and voting. In this section I will showcase some examples of projects that use maps to tell different election stories.

Source: Reddit

It is impossible to talk about geography in elections without talking about constituency borders. Somewhat overlooked in proportional electoral systems (like the Portuguese one), this issue becomes election-defining in systems with uninominal electoral circles. The way districts are shaped is so determinant to the results of elections that there is an incentive for malicious actors to change them for self-benefit. Gerrymandering, or the “political manipulation of electoral district boundaries with the intent to create undue advantage for a party, group, or socioeconomic class within the constituency” is a very important issue, especially in US politics.

The best didactic and interactive example I’ve seen to explain the real power of gerrymandering is The New York Times’ interactive game Can You Gerrymander Your Party to Power?. It puts the reader into the shoes of a district designer, with the sole purpose of maximizing its own election result.

Source: The New York Times

The Guardian uses scrollytelling to show how Republicans gerrymandered North Carolina’s electoral district borders to dismantle a widely democratic district diluting it into several Republican majority ones.

Source: The Guardian

The New Yorker newspaper The City published Get To Know Your City Council District where it includes a wide range of information for every city district in New York, from socio-economic data to redistricting information (as shown below).

Source: The City

Redistricting can also happen for different reasons. In the European Union, economic development funds are often allocated to regions according to their economic performance relative to the other regions, so that funds are allocated proportionally to the regions most in need. The Pudding’s Why EU Regions are Reshaping Their Borders uncovers how countries are reshaping their internal borders to maximize the amount of funds received, separating richer capital cities from their surroundings. It also happened in Portugal this year, with the creation of a new region, “Oeste e Vale do Tejo”, and the separation of the Metropolitan Area of Lisbon from Setúbal’s Peninsula.

Source: The Pudding

Finally, it is impossible not to talk about maps and elections without at least mentioning the amazing electoral results maps from El País. Due to the availability of  very granular election data, it is possible to visualize results from very small neighborhoods and cross-reference it with socio-economic data.

Source: El País

A wider-picture map also from El País, showing the evolution of European Election results in all EU countries since 1999.

Source: El País

Due to the uncommon two-round system in French Legislative elections, the withdrawal of candidates in three-way runoffs has a huge importance on the final results of the election. In the last elections, a Republican Front was established between most left-wing and centrist candidates, pushing the far-right to a much worse result than they previously expected. This ternary chart by Bloomberg shows how this unique strategy worked out in different scenarios.
